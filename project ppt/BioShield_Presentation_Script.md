**Deck:** `BioShield Presentation-print.html`
**Total slides:** 14
**Estimated speaking time:** ~14 minutes (full depth) · ~10 minutes (trimmed)

> **Scope clarification (verified from source):** This project is about
> detecting **AI-generated fake biomedical *abstracts*** — the short
> summary section of a research paper — *not* full-length papers.
> All real samples are PubMed abstracts streamed from
> `ccdv/pubmed-summarization`; all fake samples are ~256-token abstracts
> generated by BioMistral-7B or SeqGAN.

---

## One-line summary

BioShield is an experiment that asks: *"If we build an AI that catches fake
biomedical abstracts, what actually makes that AI hard to fool — better
training, smarter rewriting tricks, or just the type of fake-text generator
we test it against?"* The team ran four carefully controlled experiments
(an ablation study) to find out.

---

## Slide 1 — Title

> Good morning everyone. Our project is called **BioShield**, and it's
> about defending biomedical research from AI-generated fake abstracts.
>
> Imagine someone uses a ChatGPT-style model to fabricate a fake medical
> abstract that *looks* exactly like a real PubMed entry. A clinician
> reads it during a literature search, trusts it, and changes how they
> treat a patient. That's the danger we're trying to stop.
>
> We built a detector that tells real PubMed abstracts apart from
> AI-generated fake ones. But just building it isn't enough — we need to
> know *why* it works and *when* it breaks. So we ran four versions of
> the same experiment, each with one knob changed. We generated 5,622
> samples in total, and trained everything on a single Apple M3 Max laptop.
>
> Our team has four members — Alekhya, Pooja, Rayudu, and myself.

---

## Slide 2 — Why Biomedical Deepfake Text Matters

> Three reasons this problem is urgent.
>
> **First — clinical risk.** AI-written abstracts are now so good that
> humans can't reliably tell them apart from real ones. If a fake
> abstract enters the medical literature or a citation database, it can
> directly affect patient care.
>
> **Second — it's an arms race.** If we train a detector once and walk
> away, the next generation of AI text generators learns to slip past it.
> Robustness isn't a one-time thing; it has to keep up.
>
> **Third — most existing research targets generic AI text** — tweets,
> essays, news. Biomedical writing has its own rhythm, vocabulary, and
> structure. It deserves its own detector and its own evaluation.
>
> Our central question: **does the *type* of fake-abstract generator
> matter more than fancy training tricks?** Spoiler — yes, and the gap
> is huge.

---

## Slide 3 — Data Sources & Construction

> Our dataset is simple and balanced. **1,500 real abstracts** pulled
> from PubMed via `ccdv/pubmed-summarization`, and **1,500 fake
> abstracts** generated zero-shot by BioMistral-7B. Total: 3,000.
>
> We split it 80 / 10 / 10 — 2,400 for training, 300 for validation, and
> 300 held out for the final test. The held-out set is never seen during
> training, so the numbers we report are honest.
>
> Why 50/50? So that one metric — **F1** — can be compared cleanly
> across all four experiments without reweighting tricks.

---

## Slide 4 — Four Ablation Conditions

> This is the heart of our experiment. Same detector, same data — only
> the training conditions change.
>
> - **Condition A — Baseline.** Train once on the 3,000 abstracts. No
>   tricks. Result: nearly perfect — AUC 0.9997, F1 0.980.
>
> - **Condition B — SeqGAN + Retrain.** Replace BioMistral with an
>   older, weaker LSTM-based fake-abstract generator called SeqGAN, then
>   retrain the detector on its output for 3 rounds. Result: F1
>   collapses to 0.655. The detector misses **98.7%** of SeqGAN fakes.
>
> - **Condition C — BioMistral + Rewrite Agent.** Keep BioMistral fakes,
>   then take the *hardest* fakes (the ones the detector almost mistook
>   for real) and have a Qwen2.5 agent rewrite them to sound even more
>   reviewer-like. No retraining. Result: detector still wins, evasion
>   only 0.7%.
>
> - **Condition D — Full Pipeline.** Same as C, but *also* retrain the
>   detector on those rewrites. Result: identical to C — 0.7% evasion.
>
> So C and D give the *exact same numbers*. That's a clue, not a bug —
> we'll come back to it.

---

## Slide 5 — Full System Architecture

> Here's the whole pipeline, end-to-end.
>
> 1. **Inputs.** Real PubMed abstracts, model weights, and a
>    cross-domain benchmark called RAID.
> 2. **Data Preparation.** A single Python script cleans, deduplicates,
>    and produces our train / val / test splits — 3,000 rows, 50/50.
> 3. **Training and Hardening — our core contribution.** The baseline
>    detector is trained, then the adversarial loop runs Conditions B,
>    C, or D on top, each with different generator and retraining
>    choices.
> 4. **Evaluation.** Three scripts compute AUC, F1, and evasion rate,
>    plus a cross-domain RAID benchmark.
> 5. **Outputs.** Checkpoints, JSON metrics, plots, and the paper itself.
>
> Underneath all of this is a shared support layer — YAML configs, seed
> logging, Git SHA stamps on every checkpoint — so anyone can rerun our
> experiments and get the same numbers. The whole pipeline is
> **reproducible, modular, and ablation-ready** — flipping one config
> flag switches between A, B, C, and D.
>
> Label convention: **1 = real, 0 = fake.**

---

## Slide 6 — The Adversarial Loop (Per Round)

> Now zooming into Stage 3 — what actually happens in each round of the
> loop. Three rounds total.
>
> - **Step 1 — Generate.** Produce 250 fresh fake abstracts. In
>   Condition B, SeqGAN generates them. In C and D, BioMistral does.
> - **Step 2 — Score and Rank.** The detector reads each fake and
>   outputs a probability that it's real. We sort them — the fakes the
>   detector almost fell for go to the top.
> - **Step 3 — Rewrite *(C & D only)*.** Take the hardest 25% — about
>   62 abstracts — and feed them to Qwen2.5-7B with the prompt *"rewrite
>   this to sound like a biomedical reviewer."* Condition B skips this.
> - **Step 4 — Retrain *(B & D only)*.** Add the new fakes to the
>   training data and retrain the detector for 2 more epochs. Condition
>   C skips this.
> - **Step 5 — Evaluate.** Score AUC, F1, and evasion rate.
>
> Then loop back to Step 1. Three full rounds. The condition determines
> which steps run.

---

## Slide 7 — BioMistral-7B (Fake Abstract Generator)

> BioMistral is our fake-abstract generator for Conditions A, C, and D.
> It's a 7-billion-parameter language model — same architecture as
> Mistral, but **continued-pretrained on PubMed Central**, so it speaks
> fluent biomedical English.
>
> We fine-tuned it for 3 epochs with cross-entropy loss — basically
> training it to predict the next word of real PubMed abstracts. To fit
> on the laptop, we used **bfloat16 precision** and **gradient
> checkpointing**, which dropped peak memory from 28 GB to about 16 GB.
>
> At sampling time we used temperature 0.9 and top-p 0.95 — moderately
> creative settings. About 7.8 seconds per abstract on the M3 Max.

---

## Slide 8 — SeqGAN (Out-of-Family Baseline)

> SeqGAN is the deliberate weak link — used *only* in Condition B. It's
> a small LSTM-based, word-level generator. We trained it just with
> teacher forcing and skipped the full GAN reinforcement-learning step.
> The point isn't to make a great fake-abstract generator — it's to test
> what happens when the detector faces abstracts from a *completely
> different generator family*.
>
> The result is striking: evasion stays at about 99% across all 3 rounds
> of retraining. SeqGAN's text is so different from real biomedical
> English that the detector's decision boundary just doesn't cover it.
> Even after retraining on SeqGAN samples, it can't catch new ones.

---

## Slide 9 — BiomedBERT-Large (The Detector)

> This is the detector — held constant across all four conditions, so
> any difference in results is purely about the experimental setup.
>
> It's BiomedBERT-Large — 340 million parameters, three times bigger
> than the more common PubMedBERT-base. Pretrained on PubMed abstracts,
> so its built-in vocabulary already understands medical terminology. We
> added a simple binary classification head on top: real or fake.
>
> Training: 2 epochs per round, batch size 16, max sequence length 512
> tokens, bfloat16, on Apple's MPS GPU backend. Cross-entropy loss,
> AdamW optimizer, with a 10% warmup and linear decay.
>
> On the baseline, it scores **0.9997 AUC** and **0.980 F1** —
> essentially perfect against BioMistral's fake abstracts.

---

## Slide 10 — Qwen2.5-7B (Adversarial Rewrite Agent)

> Qwen2.5-7B is our adversarial rewriter — used in Conditions C and D.
> Its job is to take the fake abstracts the detector found suspicious
> and rewrite them to be more convincing.
>
> Each round, we sort 250 fakes by how 'real' the detector thinks they
> are. We grab the top 25% — the 62 hardest cases — and Qwen rewrites
> them with the prompt *"sound like a biomedical reviewer wrote it."*
> The rest pass through untouched.
>
> But here's the catch — Qwen's rewrites **didn't help**. Evasion stayed
> at 0.7% from round 2 onward. Why? Because the detector was already
> maxed out — it was catching BioMistral fakes nearly perfectly, so
> there was no room left for an adversary to push down. The honest
> finding is that the rewrite agent had nothing to fix.

---

## Slide 11 — Evaluation Metrics

> Three metrics, each measuring something different.
>
> - **AUC — Area Under the ROC Curve.** The probability the detector
>   ranks a random fake below a random real. 0.5 is random guessing,
>   1.0 is perfect. We use it because it's threshold-free and the gold
>   standard in medical AI.
> - **F1 — harmonic mean of precision and recall.** Penalizes both
>   missed fakes and false alarms equally. Comparable across conditions
>   because our 50/50 split keeps it well-defined.
> - **Evasion Rate (ER) — the headline number.** The fraction of fake
>   abstracts that slip through and get labeled as real. This is what an
>   attacker cares about. Low evasion = strong detector.

## Slide 12 — Ablation Conditions: Full Scoreboard

> Here's everything in one table.
>
> | Condition | Test AUC | Test F1 | Acc | Final Evasion |
> |---|---|---|---|---|
> | A — Baseline | 0.9997 | 0.980 | 0.98 | — (no loop) |
> | B — SeqGAN | 0.9498 | 0.655 | 0.49 | **0.987** |
> | C — Agent only | 0.9993 | 0.980 | 0.98 | 0.007 |
> | D — Full | 0.9993 | 0.980 | 0.98 | 0.007 |
> | RAID (cross-domain) | — | — | — | 0.980 |
>
> Two things to notice. **First**, generator family matters massively.
> SeqGAN's 99% evasion versus BioMistral's 0.7% is a **100× spread**.
> **Second**, C and D produce identical numbers — that's because the
> baseline already hits validation AUC 0.999, the ceiling. When we use
> 'best-validation-AUC' to pick checkpoints, the loop just keeps rolling
> back to round-zero weights. So adversarial retraining never gets a
> chance to show its effect. That's an *evaluation design* lesson, not
> a code bug.
>
> One more row — RAID, a cross-domain benchmark with GPT-4 / Claude
> output. Our detector misses 98% of those — expected, since we never
> trained on those generators.

---

## Slide 13 — Key Finding: Generator-Family Dependence Dominates

> If you take one thing from this talk, take this. **Generator family
> dominates everything else.**
>
> - SeqGAN: **99%** evasion
> - BioMistral: **0.7%** evasion
> - RAID (GPT-4 / Claude): **98%** evasion
>
> Same detector, same training procedure — only the generator family
> changed, and evasion swings by **two orders of magnitude**.
>
> That means **a published deepfake-abstract detector must declare which
> generator family it was trained against.** Saying "our detector
> catches AI-generated medical text" is meaningless without that
> disclosure.
>
> Our high RAID evasion isn't a weakness — it's a *positioning
> statement*. We built a BioMistral-family detector. Other generator
> families are a separate research problem.

## Slide 14 — What We Built, What We Learned

> Three claims we can defend, and four next steps.
>
> **Three Defensible Claims:**
>
> 1. Biomedical abstract detectors are **generator-family bound**.
>    100× spread in evasion at our scale.
> 2. Picking checkpoints by validation AUC **hides the retraining
>    signal** when the baseline is already at ceiling. C = D taught us
>    this — it's an evaluation-design lesson, not a pipeline bug.
> 3. **Cross-domain transfer is open.** RAID's 98% evasion is the next
>    problem, not a flaw.
>
> **Next Steps to Publish:**
>
> 1. **Harder generator** — LoRA-fine-tune BioMistral specifically to
>    evade the detector via DPO or reward-weighted fine-tuning. That
>    breaks the C=D ceiling problem.
> 2. **Better checkpoint criterion** — replace 'best-val-AUC' with
>    'best-evasion-on-held-out-pool' so checkpoint choice is
>    adversarially aware.
> 3. **Transfer attack** — populate `transfer_test.csv` with Llama-3.2
>    fakes. Second cross-family data point completes the generator
>    matrix.
> 4. **Scale up** — Tier 1: 4k abstracts overnight (~16 h on M3 Max).
>    Tier 2: 10k abstracts (~65 h) for a publishable run.
>
> Thank you. Happy to take questions.

## Quick-Reference Glossary (for the Q&A)

- **Abstract** — the ~250-word summary section at the top of a research
  paper. Our entire dataset is abstracts, not full papers.
- **Ablation** — controlled experiment that turns one component on/off
  to measure its contribution.
- **AUC** — Area Under the ROC Curve. Threshold-free ranking metric.
- **Evasion Rate** — fraction of fake abstracts that fool the detector.
- **In-family / out-of-family** — whether the test-time fake generator
  shares architecture / training with the one used during training.
- **MPS** — Apple's Metal Performance Shaders GPU backend for PyTorch.
- **LoRA** — Low-Rank Adaptation, a parameter-efficient fine-tuning
  method. Wired in but not activated for this run.
